Looking Glass

Overview

Client & Duration

Metro21:Smart Cities Institute, Seven weeks (Fall 2019)

Collaborators

Aaron Bishop, Aditi Magal, Emily Yang, Haley Park

Primary Role

User Researcher: co-designed research studies and synthesis activities
Prototyping Lead: utilized a lazer cutter to create a prototype

We were asked to help the City of Pittsburgh better engage citizens with its memorial and public art programs -- sharing knowledge about those works and allowing active participation.

Challenge

How might we increase engagement around public art by creating a more dynamic and expressive experience for residents?

We recognized that one of the reasons why interest in public art is low is that people who have already seen the piece are less motivated to engage with the art when the content / conversation surrounding the public art is static. One way we thought to tackle this problem space was to encourage creative engagement and dynamic interactions.

Solution

Looking Glass is an interactive board placed near a public art installation that viewers of public art can draw on to decorate the art, write on to interpret the piece or comment on others' drawings and to use to take photographs of the augmented view.

It has four core pillars:

Collaborative

An inclusive way for local residents to collectively create and generate thoughts and opinions about the public art piece

Transparent

An augmented way for local residents to see through a lens where they can contribute to the art without modifying the original piece

Dynamic

A novel way to see the art, to inspire new ways to interpret the art and maintain engagement with residents

Physical

A salient way to attract people to engage with the medium by providing a tangible device at the location of the art

Approach

Contextual Inquiry

Observed if/ how people currently engage with public art and learn what their experiences are

  • Visited 3 installations and talked to 2 types of people: those who stopped by the art, and those who didn't
  • Gathered data in both the weekday and the weekend about why they were in the neighborhood

Affinity Diagram

Transcribed 8 interviews and grouped similar themes together. We found:

  • People don't plan structured activity around public art; more an in-the-moment action than premeditated
  • Engagement increases in areas with high foot traffic (i.e. bus stops)
  • People believe that engagement can be passive (simply noticing it in the background)

Worst Possible Idea

Ideated a variety of possible solutions without any contraints for 15 minutes, had ideas around:

  • Allowing people to share their opinion/ interpretation of the art
  • Incorporting different senses (i.e. audio, smell)
  • Changing the location of public art
  • Encourage engagement through incentives (i.e. material, psychological)

Speed Dating

Created and showed storyboards of ideas derived from Worst Possible Idea:

  • People didn't view modification of public art as vandalism if it didn't subvert the artist’s intention
  • People only wanted to express their experiences/ opinions anonymously
  • Incorporating social media was deemed distasteful and hand-held devices in general were seen as a distraction
  • Guided experience to approaching art (i.e. walkway/ viewer platform) intrigued people; serves as a short and low-effort activity

Think Aloud Protocol

Since allowing citizens to modify art installments most directly addressed the static nature of public art, we wanted to explore this idea further. Augmented Reality (AR) was identified as a potential tool that could allow anyone to easily modify the art, and we wanted to determine the suitability of AR for creating a new dimension for people to interact with public art

  • Explored WallaMe, an app that enables users to hide and share digital meesages that are embedded in the real world
  • Asked 6 people to 1) make a post, 2) react to a post, 3) find information from posts
  • Wanted to learn how quickly people learned to use the app, how many people could complete tasks, and overall, how interested they were in making & viewing posts
  • No one intuitively figured out how the AR part of the app worked; they did not understand that they need to explore their environment with the phone camera to discover messages

Revised Storyboard

Because none of the participants understood how AR worked, at least through a mobile app, we decided to simply take the virtual notion of AR and make it physical. Insights from the speeddating sessions also revealed that phones were generally seen as distractions from art, so this change was a step in the right direction. The new idea combined two concepts from the storyboards: that of a guided experience to approaching the art (i.e. indicating where to stand when observing the art) and the concept of soliciting reflections and contributions from passersby.

Experience Prototyping

Tested our protoype to determine if people want to engage with public art and share their contribution. Our prototype is a stand with a clear acrylic board attached at eye-level. It’s placed in front of a public art piece and people can draw on the acrylic board to decorate the view of the public art.

The stand includes a message to the user that invites the user to draw on the acrylic board and to, if desired, erase any drawings already on the board.

Furthermore, the stand includes instructions to the user on how to post his/her work to an Instagram account created by the research team. The account (@pghpublicart_innovation) is dedicated to participatory public art in Pittsburgh.

SleepU

Overview

Lab & Duration

Smart Sensing for Humans Lab, Feb 2019 - May 2020

Collaborators

Julian Ramos PhD Candidate, Carolyn Zhong Research Assitant

Primary Role

Research Participant Recruiter: screened and onboarded participants
User Researcher: conducted research studies and synthesis activities

Challenge

What information and features do people need to understand and improve their daily habits, sleep habits, and sleep quality?

There are currently a lot of different tools on the market for people to track and understand their sleep - to an extent. From a Fitbit on your wrist tracking your hours of sleep vs. restlessness to the Health app on your iPhone tracking your daily activity and sleep, these apps can tell you your sleep quantity and quality isn’t great but nothing on how to improve it. There are a lot of different factors that affect sleep, like caffeine intake, exercise, screen usage, and more, and by considering these alongside sleep quantity, we set out to build a tool that can help people understand and optimize their sleep quality.

The Project

SleepU is a sleep-intervention app that uses Fitbit data and user-inputed habit trackings to provide personal sleep recommendations. These help users adjust their daily behaviors and sleep environment to optimize their sleep quality.

Render

Overview

Client & Duration

Pennsylvania Prison Society, Fifteen weeks (Spring 2020)

Collaborators

Elliot Allard, Jiayi Zhao, Jennifer Kong, Zoe Lin

Primary Role

Client Liason: point of contact for both the client and faculty advisor
User Test Coordinator: prepared and lead usability research

Pennsylvania Prison Society (PPS) is a non-profit that advocates for incarcerated individuals by hearing their concerns/ problems (via "requests") and communicating their needs to the Warden. We were asked to help PPS in the development of a workflow system to be utilized by over 250+ Offcial Volunteers (OVs) across the state.

Challenge

How might we streamline communication between the central office and regional chapters to increase transparency?

The organization has 43 chapters throughout Pennsylvania, and each functions differently due to various factors such as size of the chapter, number of requests, and relationship with the prison. Because of this, there isn't a standardized way for the central office to receive regular updates from individual chapters which hinders the organization from recognizing their impact as a whole.

Solution

Render is a web-based request managing system where requests can be uploaded and delegated to OVs, updated with actionable items post-visit, and analyzed to identify common problems and trends. To make visit reports as convinient as possible, we also propose that an Interactive Voice Response (IVR) be created.

It is designed on four central ideas:

Central Hub

A system that allows documents to be easily shared and accessed by chapters and the central office

Access Levels

A required log in that indicates which features and information are relevant to the individual, based on their role in the organization

Request Analysis

A summary of the type of requests the organization receives to notice trends of problems across the state to tackle systemic issues

Minimalist Interface

A clean and simple design that is straightforward for anyone to use, especially those who aren't well experienced with technology

Approach

Semi-Structured Interview

Learned how the organization functions, from the role of different stakeholders and the intake of requests

  • Spoke to the Prison Monitoring Director and Director of Volunteers (also formerly incarcerated)
  • Created a stakeholder map with color-coded relations to represent the different methods of communication

Stakeholder Map

Synthesized information and visually represented the interactions between stakeholders, and identified pain points:

  • Too many channels and methods of receiving requests (can't be helped)
  • Results in requests getting lost due to numerous redirects
  • Reporting request updates is confusing/complex, not always clear who to send it to

Contextual Inquiry

Shadowed OVs on their visit to a prison and observed a chapter meeting. Key insights include:

  • There are two ways of delegating requests: based on the type of request, a volunteer of a specific demographic (i.e. profession, gender) will be assigned vs whoever has the capacity and can quickly address it
  • OVs heavily rely on memory when discussing previous similar requests and their consequences, request history of a specific inmate, etc

User Journey Map

Focused on the experiences of an OV and the prosses they go through when they are assigned to address a request. Identified more pain points:

  • The prison restricts OVs from bringing any objects into the facility, which prohibits note-taking
  • There isn't a database that can be used as a resource to look up relevant requests
  • There isn't a standard report format, so OVs don't know what information to include

Crazy 8

Brainstormed 8 possible solutions in 8 minutes to quickly ideate features and systems. Ideas were centered around:

  • Analysis: the ability to filter through requests and analyze them and find insights and statistics
  • Unification: documenting all intake and assignment of requests, and reports of visitiations
  • Efficiency: simplify and standardize reporting, to make it as convinent as possible

Speed Dating

Created storyboards and asked participants to envision using the proposed tool in given scenario. Recorded their thoughts:

  • Ran sessions with the Prison Monitoring Director (forwards requests to specific chapters) and 4 OVs
  • Received feedback that some individuals would wish to remain anonymous because some requests are very private matters
  • Need to handle requests that are informational, and don't require a visitation

Initial Design

Created a web-based database for chapter conveners to upload, assign, and update requests

Think Aloud Protocol

Determined the usability of the web interface by asking 3 Conveners to perform tasks. Modified the prototype:

  • Confusing to have "Upload Report" and "Complete Request"; combined the actions
  • Request List was cluttered; to prioritize reporting and assigning, updated landing page to only contain active requests
  • Created "Past Requests" where OVs can filter through completed requests
  • Added column that indicates when the Central Office has seen the report, to assure OVs the value of their work

Wizard of Oz

Created a script and simulated an Interactive Voice Response for OVs to report via phone call

  • 3 OVs were asked to act like they were interacting with a voicemail system to report a visit
  • OVs seemed annoyed of the pace; decided to combine short questions to reduce the number of confirmation of answers
  • OVs would share all information at once and would later have to repeat themselves when the report came to that section; reorganized the order to have more general questions (i.e. next steps) in the front and then put shorter/ logistical questions at end (i.e. request type)
